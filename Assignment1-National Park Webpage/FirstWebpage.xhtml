Significance of Purnululu National Park and it's Nomination as a UNESCO Site

Purnululu National Park contains a cone karst formation of sandstone that is not only unique globally, but also not completely understood by geologists. Its extremely beautiful scenic views makes it attractive to the locals and tourists. The Gija, (also spelt Gidja and Kija) are aboriginal people also used to live in the reserve for almost 40,000 years. The site covers almost 240,000 Hectares of area of wildness. The cone karst have eroded for more than 20 million years that evolved into beautiful geological interest. The beehive shaped cones are around 820 feet high and covered with striped orange and black shells. As for protection and management, Purnululu is closed during wet season for the safety of tourists and locals. While wildfires are also controlled and prevented strictly.

Purnululu Park satisfied the criterion of being a UNESCO site. Its remains are relatively inaccessible, while its ability to attract tourists is also a key factor. The diversity in wildlife and plants were also considered as the conservation of these species have been conserved in an outstanding fashion. The cone karst formation stands out from any other in the world due to the geological, biological, erosional and climatic phenomena; The formation is still isn't understood by scientists. Moreover, Purnululu holds a strong integrity when it comes to preserving different species and land itself. Its strict measures and incorporation in security enhances both the park and the environment. It also gives tourists an alluring experience. With these factors, Purnululu successfully categorized as a UNESCO site.
